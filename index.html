About Me

I never considered myself a creative person because I thought that meant I could paint, sculpt, or make really pretty things out of dried macaroni. But then I learned software development, and that at the end of the day, I get to solve problems and wrangle data in a creative way.

I became interested in software development when working with a talented development/design team at Bridge. I saw the level of growth and challenge they experienced in their roles and decided I was ready to leave the management track and start at the bottom, learning a completely new skill.

Education and Experience


Nashville Software School

Junior Instructor

April 2018 - Present

I help other adults make the career change into software development, acting as both an instructor and mentor. Students often come to NSS with little to no technical background (myself included!) and my goal as a Jr Instructor is to act as an advocate and a voice for students.

During my time as a Jr Instructor, I have taught 3 months of front-end fundamentals, 6 months of Python & Django, and 6 months of C# & ASP.NET.

I support the lead instructor by teaching and reviewing concepts, as well as model how to:

  • ask good questions in class (or asking questions students didn't think to ask)
  • effectively ask for help when they are stuck (in particular, explaining what they are trying to do, what they've tried/researched, and what they think might be the issue with their code)
  • improve Googling skills (I often Google with students next to me, either one on one or in their group projects; I then explain how I sift through Stackoverflow articles to find posts that will hopefully help solve the problem)

I also noticed that graduates often didn't know how to continue learning software development as they searched for a job, so I created a graduate study guide that covers topics such as: language specific concepts (Python & C#), software development concepts (such as Object Oriented Programming principles), and suggested ways to "keep on coding".
